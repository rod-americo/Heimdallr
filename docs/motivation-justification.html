Why This Matters Now

Radiology is constrained less by scanner physics and more by workflow friction: missing priors, fragmented systems, overload, and follow-up leakage.[1][5] The 2026 opportunity is not a single model, but an orchestrated pipeline where logistics, triage, reporting, and navigation are engineered as one system.[3]

PACS/RIS/EHR orchestration LLM/VLM with governance De-identification by design Closing-the-loop follow-up

Core Definitions

Friction is implementation-in-production effort (integration, data quality, validation, governance, and operational change). It is not model complexity alone.

Navigation means active follow-up monitoring by service staff, with owner, due date, status, and escalation until closure.

Strategic Positioning

This work intentionally positions the project and its author in the Applied Software Engineering frontier of healthcare AI: execution-focused, clinically aware, and operationally measurable.

  • Personal brand: build credibility as an Applied Software Engineer for high-impact medical workflows.
  • Collaboration magnet: attract engineers, radiologists, data scientists, and compliance partners.
  • Return paths: enable academic publications, open collaboration, and sustainable commercialization.

Execution Lens

The project is positioned as a production-grade radiology operations layer, not a model showcase. Every module is framed by measurable operational value and governance constraints from day one.

Delivery Logic

Prioritize low-friction infrastructure and security foundations before high-friction autonomy.

Clinical Logic

Expand automation only where human oversight, auditability, and rollback remain explicit.

Reference Architecture (Motivation to Implementation)

Layer Technical Focus Primary Outcome Maturity
Logistics Automation HL7/DICOM triggers, prefetch engines, cloud-native DICOMweb routing.[7][11] Priors ready at open, lower latency, less manual retrieval. Near-term
Workflow Orchestration Fair assignment, SLA-aware queues, dynamic reprioritization.[13][14] Higher throughput and equitable workload. Near-term
AI Critical Triage Critical finding flagging and urgency-first worklist reordering.[16] Reduced time-to-open for high-acuity cases. Clinical acceleration
Opportunistic Quantification Liver, bone, and emphysema biomarkers from routine CT.[20][25][26] Population-health value without extra scan burden. Clinical acceleration
CAC-DRS Classification Structured coronary calcium classification for eligible CT studies. Cardiovascular risk stratification integrated into pre-report flow. Clinical acceleration
LLM/VLM Reporting Copilot Structured drafting, style adaptation, drift controls.[29][30] Faster reports with controlled quality boundaries. Clinical acceleration
De-Identification Gateway Metadata redaction + burned-in text removal + pseudonymization.[35][37] Safe external API usage and compliance readiness. Near-term
Patient Navigation NLP extraction of recommendations and follow-up tracking.[41][44] Closing the loop and reducing lost follow-up risk. Scale-up
Urology Navigation Active monitoring of urology navigable findings (stones, Bosniak III/IV, solid renal/adrenal/ureter/bladder/prostate/testicular findings). Timely specialty follow-up; suspected neoplasms are also routed to Oncology Navigation. Scale-up
Agentic Frontier Multi-agent workflow coordination, digital twins, federated learning.[47][48][49] Strategic differentiation and next-gen AI operations. Long-term

Call for Collaborators

Heimdallr is open to contributors who want to build measurable radiology infrastructure, not demo-only AI. Collaboration is especially welcome in:

  1. HL7/FHIR + DICOM interoperability engineering
  2. Clinical validation protocols and safety evaluations
  3. LLM guardrails, red-teaming, and governance automation
  4. De-identification and API security hardening
  5. Outcomes analytics for publication and operational ROI
